Abstract

Self-Protecting Systems rely on an autonomic manager to coordinate threat detection and response; however,
this centralization introduces a fundamental vulnerability: if the manager is compromised, the entire system’s
security may be undermined. Previous work has proposed mitigating this risk by implementing the manager as a
permissioned blockchain with BFT consensus and smart contracts. In this paper, we argue that the choice of
blockchain technology is not neutral and should be derived from a careful analysis of the SPS application context,
since its requirements may differ significantly from those of typical blockchain applications. We identify these
requirements and present an initial comparison between a conventional choice (Quorum, a fork of Ethereum) and
a requirement-driven alternative (CometBFT). We provide preliminary experimental results for both approaches.

1. Introduction
Self-Protecting Systems (SPS) are autonomic systems that detect and mitigate cyber threats with limited
or no human intervention [1]. In the canonical SPS architecture (see Figure 1), a managed system is
supervised by an autonomic manager responsible for monitoring, detection, and response. Although
effective in many settings, this design also introduces a structural vulnerability: an adversary may target
the autonomic manager itself, compromising its ability to detect attacks and coordinate responses [2].
In SPS research, protecting the component that protects the system remains a central and still largely
open challenge. Prior work [3] addressed this weakness by implementing the autonomic manager as
a permissioned blockchain. In that approach, alerts generated by Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)
are submitted as transactions to a blockchain operated by multiple system nodes and recorded in the
state of a smart contract. The underlying consensus mechanism provides a consistent, system-wide
view of the monitored state. The same contract also determines which mitigation actions should be
executed and when. Actuator nodes then retrieve these decisions from the blockchain and enforce the
corresponding actions. The key idea for improving the protection of the autonomic manager is that
consensus tolerates a bounded number of compromised replicas; therefore, the manager can continue
to operate correctly even under partial compromise. Moreover, the smart-contract logic can be designed
to mitigate malicious inputs (e.g., by requiring corroboration from multiple independent alerts before
triggering a response).
We stress that this is a rather atypical use of blockchain technology, and we argue that, in this specific
setting, selecting the underlying blockchain platform is not a neutral choice. In [3], Quorum is adopted
as a natural baseline choice. However, despite being a well-established technology for permissioned
settings, it is not automatically a good choice in our specific context.

2. An Architecture for a Blockchain-Based Self Protecting System
Our reference architecture is based on the one proposed in [3], which follows the classic autonomic
computing paradigm composed of a managed system and a manager system [5, 1]. Both systems consist
of sets of containerized components deployed on physical or virtual hosts. A comprehensive view of
the architecture is shown in Figure 1.
The managed system comprises application containers whose behavior is characterized by monitored
state variables. The set of these variables defines the monitored system state 2 [6] that is affected by
some security event 1 .
The manager system comprises IDSs 3 , actuators 8 , and blockchain nodes 4 and 7 , each deployed
in a separate container. In the original paper [3], blockchain nodes are Quorum nodes [7]. Together, the
blockchain and the actuators constitute the Intrusion Response System (IRS). Each IDS and each actuator
is associated with a blockchain node that enables interaction with the blockchain, while additional
blockchain nodes may be deployed to increase resilience.
The core idea is that the blockchain, through a smart contract, maintains a shared state 5 across
IDSs and actuators. This shared state includes a representation of the monitored system state, as
well as information about the internal state of the IRS. More precisely, the smart contract implements
finite-state-machine logic and must be realized correctly and securely to guarantee integrity [8, 9].
IDSs submit transactions reporting their view of one or more monitored variables. Because IDSs
themselves may be compromised, the smart contract updates the shared state only through a consensusbased voting mechanism, enabling the system to tolerate the compromise of up to one-third of the
sensors [10]. For this reason, each variable should ideally be monitored by at least three heterogeneous
IDSs. Whenever the shared state is updated, the smart contract applies the response logic 6 , namely a
state–action map used to determine the appropriate countermeasure.

3. Reasoned Requirements for a Blockchain Technology in the
Context of a Cyber Resilient Self-Protecting System
The requirements in Table 1 follow directly from the role of the blockchain in the proposed architecture.
The blockchain is not intended as an open network or as a platform for general-purpose decentralized
applications. Its sole functions are (1) to gather from IDSs a shared state, (2) to ensure a reliable
functioning of the response logic even in the presence of Byzantine faults and (3) to communicate to
the actuators the decisions taken by the response logic. Under this assumption, many features typical of
public blockchains, or even of private ones, become irrelevant, while a limited set of properties becomes
essential.
The properties classified as not needed are those tied to openness and economic regulation. Gas,
fees, token incentives, and reward mechanisms serve to control and motivate unknown participants in
public systems, but they have no purpose in a permissioned environment with known nodes. These
mechanisms should not simply be neutralized: if they remain in the platform, they still add complexity,
validation burden, and attack surface. The same applies to proof-of-work, proof-of-stake, dynamic
peer discovery, fixed-interval block production, and general-purpose virtual machines. In this setting,
membership is fixed, consensus is naturally Byzantine fault tolerant, activity is driven by alerts rather
than constant transaction flow, and only a limited deterministic state machine is required. Anything

The desirable properties are those that improve efficiency without being strictly necessary. Since
the shared state is small, in-memory replication is attractive and viable. Low and predictable latency
is important because countermeasures must be triggered promptly. The ability to limit or disable
transaction history may be an important requirement in certain settings, such as low-cost embedded
systems, where maintaining an extensive record may be unnecessary or even undesirable. This is
particularly relevant in the context of SPS, despite the fact that blockchain technologies inherently
provide an immutable audit trail that can support accountability and forensic analysis. Finally, pushbased notifications can further reduce reaction time.
The required properties are few and clear: Byzantine fault-tolerant consensus with strong finality,
static permissioned membership, cryptographic transaction signing, and event-driven block production.
Together, these properties ensure that the system maintains a single authoritative state, authenticates
all actions, and progresses according to alerts and monitored-state changes rather than periodic timing.
Overall, Table 1 shows that the relevant blockchain properties in this domain are not openness,
tokenization, or generic programmability, but minimality, determinism, and Byzantine resilience.
One can argue whether regular blockchain technologies fit our needs. The answer is yes, but only
partially. Regarding required properties, the only one that is not common is “event-driven block
production” (e.g., not supported by Quorum but supported by Fabric). Regarding desired properties,
permissioned blockchains tend to favor low latency and high throughput over other features, but they
also tend to maintain the system state in secondary storage, often through an external DBMS, in order
to support arbitrarily large state. Although caching mechanisms can mitigate part of the associated
cost, this design still introduces non-negligible overhead. Some exceptions do exist. For instance, Iroha
v2 provides native in-memory storage without relying on an external DBMS, while CometBFT does

not impose a specific storage architecture and can therefore be integrated with an in-memory state

Scalability is a more critical point. The BFT family of consensus algorithms has high 𝑂(𝑛2 ) communication complexity (with 𝑛 being the number of nodes that participate in the consensus). For this
reason, they are normally used with a small number of nodes. To address this issue, some permissionless
blockchain systems adopt committee-based techniques. In particular, Algorand and Ethereum, after the
introduction of Casper FFG, rely on the random selection of a small subset of nodes to participate in
each consensus round. This approach reduces the number of effective participants per round, thereby
improving efficiency, while statistically preserving security guarantees comparable to those obtained
when all nodes are involved. While a similar argument can be made for permissioned blockchains, to
the best of our knowledge this approach is not adopted by any of the commonly used permissioned
blockchain technologies. One exception is Algorand itself, which is sometimes used in permissioned
settings, but it brings with it a number of properties that we classified as non-needed.

4. A High Level Comparison Between Quorum and CometBFT for SPS
In this section, we compare Quorum and CometBFT in the context of their application within SPS.
Quorum, selected in [3], was identified as the blockchain technology for the architecture outlined
in Section 2, making it a natural initial choice in the literature. Here, we evaluate CometBFT as an
alternative to Quorum, based on a thorough assessment of blockchain properties discussed in Section 3.
Quorum inherits the full execution overhead of the Ethereum stack. Transactions must be RLPserialized, dispatched to the EVM, interpreted opcode by opcode, and subjected to gas metering at
instruction granularity. This metering loop executes unconditionally, even in permissioned deployments
where transaction fees are never collected. Consequently, every state transition incurs multiple layers
of overhead, irrespective of its intrinsic complexity.
Quorum’s current permissioning model introduces additional inefficiency. Access control is enforced
through on-chain governance contracts, which add state and execution logic that provide little practical
value in a closed consortium with fixed, known participants. Block production is similarly inefficient:
blocks are generated at fixed intervals regardless of transaction availability. This design keeps the
consensus layer active during idle periods and imposes a systematic, interval-bounded delay between
threat detection and ledger commitment.
There is a mismatch between the required properties (see Section 3 and what Quorum provides). The
SPS response logic is naturally modeled as a finite-state automaton, comprising a fixed set of states,
transitions conditioned on IDS votes, and outputs mapped to countermeasures. Although Solidity is
expressive enough to implement such logic, the EVM remains a general-purpose execution environment.
The SPS therefore exercises only a narrow subset of the platform’s capabilities while incurring the full
cost of its generality. Table 2 summarizes structural mismatches like this across the complete set of
blockchain properties considered in the SPS application context.
CometBFT follows a different design philosophy. Through its Application Blockchain Interface
(ABCI), it enforces a clear separation between the consensus engine and the application logic. In
particular, all properties identified as not required are absent from CometBFT’s core functionality and,
when needed, must be implemented at the application layer via ABCI.
Rather than representing replicated state indirectly through smart contract state, the application
explicitly specifies its states, transition function, and outputs, while the consensus layer is responsible
solely for delivering transactions to all nodes in a consistent Byzantine fault-tolerant order. In this
respect, CometBFT acts as a framework for building application-specific blockchains whose feature set
is determined entirely by the system designer. For SPS, the required state machine can be implemented
at the same architectural level at which the EVM operates in Quorum, but with substantially lower
overhead. In a native ABCI-based design, the state machine can be realized directly in Go or Rust, so that
each state transition is executed as a native function call rather than through interpreted bytecode. This
eliminates the overhead associated with EVM interpretation, serialization, and gas metering, reducing

CometBFT also adopts an event-driven block production model, whereby blocks are generated only
in the presence of pending transactions. The consensus layer therefore remains inactive during idle
periods and can react promptly when an alert is issued, a property that is especially valuable in SPS
contexts.
The primary limitation of CometBFT is that several functionalities offered natively by platforms
such as Quorum must instead be realized at the application layer. This is particularly relevant for the
programmable features summarized in Table 2. In particular, permissioned static membership is not
enforced directly by the consensus engine and therefore requires application-level governance, although
CometBFT supports this form of extensibility. Furthermore, CometBFT does not provide an inherent
scalability advantage as the number of participating nodes increases, since consensus overhead grows
with the size of the validator set. This limitation can be mitigated through committee-based approaches to
validator participation. More generally, CometBFT enables customized validator-set selection, provided
that the corresponding mechanisms are properly designed and implemented at the application layer.
Production-grade examples of such customization exist in other ecosystems, such as Cosmos, where
validator participation is constrained through stake-based selection and continuous rotation. About
configurable audit immutability, CometBFT provides native support through configurable data-retention
policies, allowing deployments to balance auditability against resource consumption. In addition, state
synchronization enables new nodes to bootstrap from verified state snapshots rather than replaying the
full transaction history. However, this mechanism still depends on application-level implementation
choices and on the availability of nodes capable of serving snapshot data.

5. Experimental Quorum vs. CometBFT Comparison
This section presents an experimental comparison between Quorum and CometBFT. To make the
comparison, we considered an SPS implementing the architecture depicted in Figure 1 and we measured
the performance of this system switching between Quorum and CometBFT as the underlying blockchain
technology. From this point onward, we refer to Quorum as GoQuorum, as the discussion moves to
implementation details and GoQuorum represents the Go-based extension of the original Quorum
platform within the broader Quorum ecosystem.

5.1. Implementation
The prototype, publicly available at [12], was designed with architectural realism in mind. Each logical
component is deployed as an isolated container, interconnected through network segments that replicate
the addressing schemes and trust boundaries of a real-world deployment. Figure 3 shows a topology,
which we emulated in Kathara [13, 14] on top of Docker, that consists of three main components: an
untrusted external zone where the attacker is, the managed system hosting the attack target, and the
management system, which includes one actuator, three different IDSs, and several nodes that realize a
blockchain that collects IDS-generated transactions and computes responses for the actuator.
The attack target is OWASP Juice Shop [15], a deliberately vulnerable web application widely adopted
in security research for this class of experiments. A gateway node forwards a copy of all inbound traffic
to three different IDS engines: Snort [16], Suricata [17], and Zeek [18], each running in promiscuous
mode performing a signature-based attack detection on the sniffed traffic. Upon detection, each IDS
forwards an alert to its dedicated blockchain node (i.e., 𝑁 𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑀 1 , 𝑁 𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑀 2 , 𝑁 𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑀 3 ) which submits
it as a signed transaction to the smart contract governing the system state kept by the blockchain.
Once a qualified majority of validators (2/3) reaches consensus and an actuation event is emitted, the
monitoring node intercepts it and dispatches the mitigation payload to the Actuator, which enforces the
countermeasure on the target via SSH. To validate the theoretical discussion above, we replicated the
experiments across the two systems considered in our comparison, namely GoQuorum and CometBFT.
The two evaluated configurations are architecturally identical and differ only in the blockchain layer.
In the GoQuorum setup, four non-validating nodes are deployed: three serve as IDS-facing gateways (i.e., 𝑁 𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑀 1 , 𝑁 𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑀 2 , 𝑁 𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑀 3 ), while one acts as the actuation monitor (i.e. 𝑁 𝑜𝑑𝑒𝐴1 ). In
addition, three QBFT validator nodes are responsible for consensus (i.e., 𝑉 𝑎𝑙𝑖𝑑𝑎𝑡𝑜𝑟𝑉 1 , 𝑉 𝑎𝑙𝑖𝑑𝑎𝑡𝑜𝑟𝑉 2 ,
𝑉 𝑎𝑙𝑖𝑑𝑎𝑡𝑜𝑟𝑉 3 ). In the CometBFT setup, these components are replaced by light nodes (i.e., 𝑁 𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑀 1 ,

𝑁 𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑀 2 , 𝑁 𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑀 3 , 𝑁 𝑜𝑑𝑒𝐴1 ) while three full nodes operate as validators under the CometBFT consensus protocol (i.e., 𝑉 𝑎𝑙𝑖𝑑𝑎𝑡𝑜𝑟𝑉 1 , 𝑉 𝑎𝑙𝑖𝑑𝑎𝑡𝑜𝑟𝑉 2 , 𝑉 𝑎𝑙𝑖𝑑𝑎𝑡𝑜𝑟𝑉 3 ).
We further observed that recording every IDS alert as an individual on-chain transaction may
introduce unnecessary overhead, as multiple temporally adjacent alerts of the same attack type can
often be captured by a single state transition. To address this, we implemented a deduplication mechanism
within the custom API interface of the member and light nodes. This mechanism serves as a middleware
layer that filters incoming IDS alerts, rejecting repetitive notifications for an attack category if the
requested state change has already been recorded in the current interval. Specifically, the interface
maintains a local cache of the last submitted state and utilizes a background worker to consolidate unique
alerts into a single transaction every blocktime (custom time interval for CometBFT). For instance,
during a high-volume DDoS attack, where hundreds of consecutive alerts may be triggered for the
same category, only the initial state change per interval is propagated to the ledger. This architectural
optimization significantly reduces on-chain transaction load and prevents mempool saturation while
preserving the amount of information recorded.

5.2. Experimental Setup
All performance tests were conducted on a dedicated machine running Ubuntu 24.04, equipped with
an AMD EPYC 9354P processor and 192 GB of RAM. Throughout the experimental trials, hardware
resource consumption was monitored via top: CPU utilization was always below 20%. For both tested
implementations, the deployment of the containers demanded approximately 10 GB of RAM without
significant usage variation.

5.3. Objectives and Results
To evaluate the performance, scalability, and resource efficiency of the proposed architecture, we
conducted a series of automated experiments measuring end-to-end latency, sustained transaction
throughput, and on-chain transaction load under controlled attack conditions. The experimental setup
emulates concurrent attacks against the target web application using a fire-and-forget strategy from
the attacker container, thereby triggering the IDS engines to generate actionable alerts. The evaluation
addresses the following research questions:
Q1. What end-to-end latency does the SPS introduce under low traffic conditions?
Q2. What transaction throughput can the SPS sustain under increasing input load?
Q3. How does the deduplication mechanism reduce on-chain transaction load as the volume of a specific
attack increases?
We addressed Questions Q1 and Q2 by comparing the performance of the system when using
GoQuorum and CometBFT. In contrast, for Question Q3, we focused only on CometBFT, since the
transaction volume is low and both blockchain layers exhibit equivalent behavior under these conditions
5.3.1. Q1: What end-to-end latency does the SPS introduce under low traffic conditions?
To quantify the latency introduced by the whole SPS execution chain, we measured the end-to-end
response time under low traffic conditions by injecting isolated alerts and repeating the experiment
100 times for each blockchain technology. This configuration measures the baseline responsiveness of
the system in the absence of sustained attack traffic or transaction bursts. For each run, latency was
computed as the time elapsed between the detection timestamp recorded by the first IDS that generated
the alert and the timestamp at which the countermeasure was executed via SSH on the juice_shop
target.

5.3.2. Q2: What transaction throughput can the SPS sustain under increasing input load?
To evaluate the maximum throughput sustainable by the SPS, we progressively increased the input
load by submitting transaction bursts ranging from 102 to 105 transactions, using up to 200 parallel
threads. For each run, we measured: (1) the number of transactions successfully committed to the
blockchain, and (2) the elapsed time between the transmission of the first packet and the commitment
of the last transaction. The objective was to assess the transaction-processing capacity of the two
blockchain technologies under increasing load, and to identify both their throughput limits and the
onset of performance degradation. In this setting, transactions were deliberately forwarded to expose
the underlying ledger to progressively larger transaction volumes. For each load level, throughput was
computed as the ratio between committed transactions and burst completion time.
Figure 5 summarizes the results from two complementary perspectives. Figure 5a reports, for
each input burst, the number of transactions successfully committed on-chain and the corresponding
throughput. Figure 5b reports the time required to complete each burst.
The results show a marked divergence between the two configurations. CometBFT scales effectively
under increasing load: the number of committed transactions grows consistently with burst size, and
throughput rises from a few hundred TPS at low load to a peak of approximately 2.5 k TPS at higher
load levels, without any transaction loss. At the same time, latency increases from seconds to tens of
seconds, reaching 63.75 s when submitting 100 K transactions.
By contrast, GoQuorum exhibits substantially weaker scalability. Throughput improves only under
moderate load, peaking at approximately 275 TPS for bursts of 20 K transactions, while latency increases
sharply from seconds to several minutes. At higher loads (> 10 K transactions), throughput collapses,

which enables the system to support a larger number of state changes.
5.3.4. CPU Consumption
During the CometBFT benchmarks (see Figure 6 and Figure 5), user-process CPU utilization fluctuated
between 9% and 14%. In contrast, GoQuorum exhibited slightly higher CPU consumption, averaging
14% and peaking at 17%. We suppose this higher resource usage is a consequence of the higher
architectural overhead inherent in Quorum.

6. Conculsions
In this work, we identified the key requirements for a secure-by-design blockchain-based SPS and
mapped them onto two substantially different technologies, namely Quorum and CometBFT. This
comparison allowed us to assess how the choice of the blockchain backend affects the ability of the same
SPS architecture to satisfy its functional and non-functional requirements. The analysis shows that,
although both technologies can support the proposed design, CometBFT provides a better architectural
fit for the target scenario and satisfies a broader set of requirements, despite some limitations that
remain unaddressed in the current prototype.
To validate these findings, we developed a realistic SPS prototype for the protection of a vulnerable
OWASP Juice Shop service and evaluated the same implementation over the two blockchain platforms,
modifying only the underlying blockchain layer. This ensured a fair comparison and allowed us to

isolate the actual impact of the blockchain technology without altering the SPS logic or the overall
system design. The experimental evaluation shows that the CometBFT-based deployment achieves
lower latency and sustains higher load, making it more suitable for security scenarios in which timely
reaction and efficient alert handling are critical.
Overall, the results indicate that the choice of the blockchain backend plays a decisive role in the
practical effectiveness of blockchain-based SPSs. In particular, they suggest that distributed state
machine approaches such as CometBFT constitute a more promising foundation than traditional
blockchain platforms for this class of systems. Future work will focus on refining the implementation
through the flexibility offered by the ABCI interface, validating the approach in more realistic attackand-defense scenarios, and extending the evaluation with larger scalability experiments.
